ACL Surgery Complications

While ACL reconstruction is generally successful, complications can occur. Understanding potential problems helps you recognize warning signs early and seek appropriate care. This guide covers the most common complications, their symptoms, and treatment approaches.

Important Note

Most ACL reconstructions are successful without major complications. This guide is meant to inform, not alarm. If you're experiencing concerning symptoms, contact your surgeon's office promptly. Early intervention typically leads to better outcomes.

Complication Overview

ACL reconstruction has a high success rate, with approximately 85-95% of patients achieving good to excellent outcomes. However, complications occur in a minority of cases.

3-10% Graft failure rate (varies by population)
4-35% Stiffness/arthrofibrosis (range in studies)
0.3-1.7% Deep infection rate
1-3% Cyclops lesion incidence

Risk factors for complications include:

  • Concomitant injuries (meniscus, other ligaments)
  • Revision surgery
  • Poor pre-operative knee motion
  • Non-compliance with rehabilitation
  • Early return to sport
  • Smoking
  • Obesity

Arthrofibrosis (Stiffness)

Arthrofibrosis is excessive scar tissue formation within the knee joint, leading to persistent stiffness and limited range of motion. It's one of the most common complications after ACL surgery.

Signs & Symptoms

  • Inability to fully straighten the knee (extension deficit)
  • Inability to fully bend the knee (flexion deficit)
  • Persistent stiffness despite therapy
  • Pain with motion at end ranges
  • Knee feels "stuck" or "blocked"
  • Plateau in progress despite consistent rehab

Risk Factors

  • Surgery too soon after injury (inflamed knee)
  • Pre-operative stiffness
  • Delayed or inadequate physical therapy
  • Concomitant MCL injury or repair
  • Individual healing response (some people scar more)

Treatment Options

Conservative

  • Aggressive physical therapy
  • Low-load prolonged stretching
  • Serial casting or dynamic splinting
  • Anti-inflammatory medications

Surgical

  • Manipulation under anesthesia (MUA)
  • Arthroscopic lysis of adhesions
  • Open debridement (severe cases)

Prevention

  • Achieve full extension before surgery (prehab)
  • Start PT early and consistently
  • Prioritize extension in early weeks
  • Don't wait to address ROM deficits

Extension is Critical

Lack of full extension is the most problematic stiffness issue. Even a few degrees of extension loss can significantly affect gait and long-term knee health. This is why early rehab focuses heavily on achieving 0 degrees (or hyperextension matching your other knee).

Graft Failure (Re-Tear)

Graft failure means the reconstructed ACL has torn again, resulting in recurrent instability. This can occur due to trauma, inadequate healing, or returning to sport too early.

Signs & Symptoms

  • Episode of giving way or instability
  • Return of instability symptoms
  • Pivot shift returns on examination
  • Positive Lachman test
  • Swelling after pivoting incident
  • Loss of confidence in the knee

Causes of Graft Failure

  • Traumatic re-injury: New injury during sport or activity
  • Premature return to sport: Before graft fully matured (typically <9 months)
  • Technical factors: Tunnel placement, graft fixation issues
  • Biological failure: Graft didn't heal or incorporate properly
  • Graft laxity: Gradual stretching over time

Risk Factors for Re-Tear

  • Young age (<20 years old)—higher activity level and risk tolerance
  • Return to cutting/pivoting sports
  • Return before 9 months
  • Not passing return-to-sport criteria
  • Previous ACL injury (either knee)
  • Allograft in young, active patients

Treatment

Graft failure typically requires revision ACL reconstruction—a more complex surgery than the original. The decision depends on:

  • Activity level and goals
  • Degree of instability
  • Cause of failure (must be addressed)
  • Associated injuries (meniscus, cartilage)

Prevention

  • Wait at least 9 months before returning to sport
  • Pass objective return-to-sport testing
  • Complete neuromuscular training program
  • Address biomechanical deficits
  • Maintain strength long-term

Re-Tear Statistics

Young athletes returning to pivoting sports have approximately 20-25% rate of ACL injury (either knee) within 2 years. This includes both graft failures and contralateral (opposite knee) ACL tears. Comprehensive rehab and meeting RTS criteria significantly reduce this risk.

Infection

Post-operative infection is rare but serious. It can be superficial (skin/wound) or deep (within the joint—septic arthritis). Deep infection threatens the graft and joint.

Signs & Symptoms

  • Increasing pain after initial improvement
  • Redness spreading from incision
  • Warmth and swelling beyond expected
  • Fever (>101°F / 38.3°C)
  • Drainage from incisions (especially if cloudy/colored)
  • Foul smell from wound
  • Feeling unwell, chills

Seek Immediate Care If:

  • Fever above 101°F (38.3°C)
  • Spreading redness around incision
  • Pus or colored discharge from wound
  • Pain dramatically worsening after day 3-4

Don't wait to call—infection requires prompt treatment.

Risk Factors

  • Diabetes
  • Obesity
  • Smoking
  • Immunosuppression
  • Previous knee surgeries
  • Prolonged surgical time

Treatment

  • Superficial: Oral antibiotics, wound care
  • Deep infection: IV antibiotics, often surgical washout (lavage)
  • Severe cases: Graft removal may be necessary (can reconstruct later)

Prevention

  • Keep incisions clean and dry as directed
  • Don't submerge in water until cleared (bath, pool)
  • Watch for warning signs daily in early weeks
  • Take prophylactic antibiotics as prescribed
  • Optimize diabetes control if applicable
  • Don't pick at scabs or incisions

Cyclops Lesion

A cyclops lesion is a localized nodule of fibrous tissue that forms in front of the ACL graft, typically in the intercondylar notch. The name comes from its appearance on MRI, resembling a single eye.

Signs & Symptoms

  • Loss of terminal extension (can't fully straighten)
  • Mechanical block to extension (feels like something is stopping it)
  • Audible or palpable clunk with extension
  • Extension loss that developed after initially achieving full extension
  • Pain at end-range extension

Causes

  • Residual graft tissue at tibial tunnel
  • Impingement of graft in the notch
  • Debris from initial injury or surgery
  • Individual healing response

Diagnosis

  • Physical exam showing mechanical extension block
  • MRI shows characteristic nodule anterior to graft

Treatment

  • Arthroscopic removal of the lesion
  • Generally straightforward surgery
  • Good outcomes—extension usually restored
  • Aggressive PT after to maintain extension

Prevention

  • Achieve full extension early in rehab
  • Surgeon technique (proper notchplasty, graft tensioning)

Blood Clots (DVT)

Deep vein thrombosis (DVT) is a blood clot in the deep veins, typically of the leg. While relatively uncommon after ACL surgery, it's a serious complication that can be life-threatening if the clot travels to the lungs (pulmonary embolism).

Signs & Symptoms of DVT

  • Calf pain or tenderness (may feel like a cramp that doesn't resolve)
  • Swelling in the calf or entire leg
  • Warmth in the affected area
  • Redness or discoloration
  • Swelling much greater in one leg than expected

Signs of Pulmonary Embolism (Emergency)

  • Sudden shortness of breath
  • Chest pain (worse with deep breath)
  • Rapid heart rate
  • Coughing up blood
  • Feeling faint or passing out

Call 911 Immediately

If you experience sudden shortness of breath, chest pain, or cough up blood—these are signs of pulmonary embolism, a medical emergency.

Risk Factors

  • History of blood clots
  • Clotting disorders
  • Prolonged immobility
  • Obesity
  • Smoking
  • Birth control pills or hormone therapy
  • Older age

Prevention

  • Early mobilization (getting up and moving)
  • Ankle pumps while lying down
  • Compression stockings if recommended
  • Blood thinners for high-risk patients
  • Staying hydrated
  • Avoiding prolonged sitting

Nerve Injury

Nerve damage can occur during ACL surgery, most commonly affecting small sensory nerves near the incisions. Major nerve injury is rare.

Types of Nerve Issues

Infrapatellar Branch of Saphenous Nerve

Most common nerve affected. Runs near the tibial harvest site (hamstring graft) and tibial tunnel.

  • Numbness on front/outer aspect of shin below knee
  • Usually permanent but not functionally significant
  • Occurs in up to 50-70% of patients to some degree

Peroneal Nerve

Rare but more serious. Runs around the outside of the knee.

  • Foot drop (difficulty lifting foot)
  • Numbness on top of foot
  • Usually from positioning or brace pressure
  • Often recovers over months

Treatment

  • Most sensory changes are permanent but tolerable
  • Motor nerve injuries often recover with time
  • Physical therapy for any weakness
  • Bracing if foot drop present (AFO)
  • Rarely, surgical exploration if no recovery

Prevention

  • Careful surgical technique
  • Proper brace padding
  • Avoiding external pressure on outer knee

Donor Site Problems

When using autograft (your own tissue), problems can occur at the site where the graft was harvested.

Patellar Tendon (BTB) Donor Site

  • Anterior knee pain: Most common issue (10-40%)
  • Kneeling pain: Discomfort with kneeling activities
  • Patellar fracture: Rare (<1%)
  • Patellar tendon rupture: Very rare
  • Patella baja: Low-riding kneecap from scarring

Hamstring Donor Site

  • Hamstring weakness: May persist 1-2 years
  • Cramping: During activity
  • Numbness: Inner knee/shin (saphenous nerve)
  • Harvest site pain: Usually resolves within months

Quad Tendon Donor Site

  • Quad weakness: Early weakness, usually recovers
  • Suprapatellar pain: Above kneecap
  • Quad tendon rupture: Very rare

Management

  • Specific rehab exercises targeting donor site
  • Time—most issues improve over 6-12 months
  • Massage and soft tissue work
  • Injection therapy in some cases

Hardware Issues

ACL reconstruction uses various fixation devices (screws, buttons, staples) to secure the graft. Occasionally, these cause problems.

Potential Issues

  • Hardware prominence: Screw or button palpable/painful under skin
  • Screw divergence: Screw migrates from tunnel (rare)
  • Hardware loosening: Usually from graft failure first
  • Irritation: Clicking or discomfort from hardware

Treatment

  • Hardware removal if symptomatic (usually after graft healed—12+ months)
  • Typically outpatient procedure
  • Not all hardware needs removal—only if causing problems

Complication Prevention Strategies

Before Surgery

  • Achieve full knee extension
  • Reduce swelling
  • Strengthen quad
  • Optimize health (quit smoking, control diabetes)
  • Wait for inflammation to settle

Early Post-Op

  • Start PT immediately
  • Prioritize extension
  • Monitor incisions for infection
  • Move to prevent blood clots
  • Follow weight-bearing restrictions

Ongoing Rehab

  • Consistent PT attendance
  • Address ROM deficits promptly
  • Progressive strengthening
  • Don't rush milestones
  • Report concerning symptoms early

Return to Sport

  • Wait at least 9 months
  • Pass objective RTS criteria
  • Complete neuromuscular training
  • Gradual return progression
  • Maintain strength long-term

When to Call Your Doctor

Call Immediately / Go to ER

  • Fever over 101°F (38.3°C)
  • Chest pain or shortness of breath
  • Severe calf pain with swelling
  • Spreading redness from incision
  • Pus or foul-smelling drainage
  • Severe pain uncontrolled by medication
  • Sudden numbness or foot drop

Call Within 24-48 Hours

  • Increasing swelling despite elevation/ice
  • Wound opening or separating
  • Clear or bloody drainage from wound
  • Unable to bear weight as expected
  • ROM significantly worse than previous visit
  • New instability or giving way

Discuss at Next Visit

  • ROM plateau despite therapy
  • Persistent clicking or catching
  • Mild donor site discomfort
  • Numbness around incisions
  • Questions about activity progression

Key Takeaways

  • Most ACL reconstructions are successful—complications are the exception, not the rule
  • Early recognition and treatment of complications leads to better outcomes
  • Arthrofibrosis is best prevented through early, consistent PT focused on extension
  • Graft failure risk is reduced by waiting at least 9 months and meeting RTS criteria
  • Infection is rare but requires immediate attention if suspected
  • Don't hesitate to contact your surgeon if something seems wrong
