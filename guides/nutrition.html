Nutrition for ACL Recovery

What you eat significantly impacts how well and how quickly you recover from ACL surgery. This guide covers the evidence-based nutritional strategies to support tissue healing, reduce inflammation, maintain muscle mass, and optimize your recovery.

Why Nutrition Matters for Recovery

After ACL surgery, your body is working hard to:

  • Heal surgical incisions and internal tissue
  • Incorporate and vascularize the new graft
  • Manage inflammation (necessary but needs to be controlled)
  • Maintain muscle mass during reduced activity
  • Support bone healing at graft attachment sites

Proper nutrition provides the raw materials for all these processes. Inadequate nutrition can slow healing, increase infection risk, and lead to greater muscle loss.

Key Nutritional Priorities

  1. Adequate protein for tissue repair and muscle preservation
  2. Sufficient calories to fuel healing (don't under-eat)
  3. Anti-inflammatory foods to manage excessive inflammation
  4. Key micronutrients that support collagen synthesis and bone healing
  5. Proper hydration for all cellular processes

Protein Requirements

Protein is critical after surgery. It provides amino acids needed for tissue repair, collagen synthesis, and preventing muscle atrophy. Research shows surgical patients need significantly more protein than normal.

How Much Protein?

Phase Protein Target Example (150 lb person)
Normal adult 0.8 g/kg body weight ~55g/day
Post-surgery recovery 1.6-2.0 g/kg body weight 110-135g/day
Active rehab phase 1.6-2.2 g/kg body weight 110-150g/day

Best Protein Sources

Animal Sources

  • Chicken breast (31g per 100g)
  • Fish/salmon (25g per 100g)
  • Lean beef (26g per 100g)
  • Eggs (6g each)
  • Greek yogurt (10g per 100g)
  • Cottage cheese (11g per 100g)

Plant Sources

  • Tofu (8g per 100g)
  • Tempeh (19g per 100g)
  • Lentils (9g per 100g cooked)
  • Chickpeas (9g per 100g)
  • Edamame (11g per 100g)
  • Quinoa (4g per 100g)

Protein Timing

  • Distribute protein across 4-5 meals/snacks (25-40g per meal)
  • Include protein at every meal
  • Have protein within 2 hours after physical therapy
  • Consider casein-rich foods (cottage cheese, milk) before bed for overnight recovery

Calorie Needs

A common mistake is drastically cutting calories because you're less active. However, healing requires significant energy. Under-eating impairs recovery and accelerates muscle loss.

Don't Under-Eat

Surgery increases metabolic rate by 15-30% during the healing phase. While you're moving less, your body is working hard internally. Severe calorie restriction can:

  • Slow wound healing
  • Increase muscle loss
  • Impair immune function
  • Reduce collagen synthesis

Calorie Guidelines

  • Weeks 0-2: Eat at or slightly below maintenance (healing is the priority)
  • Weeks 2-6: Maintenance calories as activity increases
  • Month 2+: Adjust based on activity level and body composition goals

If you're gaining unwanted fat, reduce calories slightly—but prioritize protein. If you're losing weight rapidly (more than 1-2 lbs/week), you may be under-eating.

Anti-Inflammatory Foods

Inflammation is a natural part of healing, but excessive or prolonged inflammation can impair recovery and increase pain. Certain foods help manage inflammation through various mechanisms.

Foods That Fight Inflammation

Omega-3 Rich Foods

Omega-3 fatty acids have potent anti-inflammatory effects.

  • Fatty fish (salmon, mackerel, sardines)
  • Walnuts
  • Flaxseed and chia seeds
  • Fish oil supplements

Colorful Fruits & Vegetables

Rich in antioxidants and polyphenols.

  • Berries (blueberries, strawberries, cherries)
  • Leafy greens (spinach, kale)
  • Tomatoes
  • Bell peppers
  • Beets

Herbs & Spices

Concentrated anti-inflammatory compounds.

  • Turmeric (curcumin)
  • Ginger
  • Garlic
  • Cinnamon
  • Rosemary

Healthy Fats

Support anti-inflammatory pathways.

  • Extra virgin olive oil
  • Avocados
  • Nuts (almonds, walnuts)
  • Seeds

Tart Cherry Juice

Research shows tart cherry juice may reduce inflammation and muscle soreness. Consider 8-12 oz of tart cherry juice daily, especially during intense rehab phases. Choose 100% juice without added sugar.

Key Vitamins & Minerals

Several micronutrients play specific roles in tissue healing and should be prioritized during recovery.

Vitamin C

Why: Essential for collagen synthesis. Your graft and healing tissues need collagen.

Sources: Citrus fruits, bell peppers, strawberries, broccoli, kiwi

Target: 500-1000mg/day during recovery

Vitamin D

Why: Supports bone healing, immune function, and muscle function. Many people are deficient.

Sources: Sunlight, fatty fish, fortified foods, supplements

Target: 2000-4000 IU/day (get levels tested if possible)

Calcium

Why: Critical for bone healing at graft attachment sites.

Sources: Dairy, fortified plant milks, leafy greens, canned fish with bones

Target: 1000-1200mg/day

Zinc

Why: Wound healing, immune function, protein synthesis.

Sources: Oysters, beef, pumpkin seeds, chickpeas, chicken

Target: 15-30mg/day during recovery

Vitamin A

Why: Cell growth, immune function, wound healing.

Sources: Sweet potatoes, carrots, spinach, eggs

Target: 700-900 mcg RAE/day

Iron

Why: Oxygen delivery to healing tissues. May be depleted from surgical blood loss.

Sources: Red meat, spinach, lentils, fortified cereals

Note: Don't supplement unless deficient (test first)

Supplements

While whole foods should be the foundation, certain supplements may support recovery. Always discuss supplements with your healthcare provider, especially if taking medications.

Collagen Peptides

Moderate Evidence

May support collagen synthesis in tendons and ligaments. Take with vitamin C.

Dose: 10-15g/day

Timing: 30-60 min before PT with vitamin C

Vitamin D3

Strong Evidence

Essential if deficient. Supports bone, muscle, and immune function.

Dose: 2000-4000 IU/day (based on blood levels)

Omega-3 Fish Oil

Moderate Evidence

Anti-inflammatory effects. May reduce muscle loss during immobilization.

Dose: 2-3g EPA+DHA combined daily

Creatine

Emerging Evidence

May help preserve muscle mass during reduced activity. Well-studied for safety.

Dose: 5g/day

Curcumin (Turmeric Extract)

Moderate Evidence

Anti-inflammatory properties. Look for formulations with enhanced absorption.

Dose: 500-1000mg/day (as curcumin)

Glucosamine/Chondroitin

Limited Evidence

Traditional joint supplement. Evidence for ACL recovery is limited but may help cartilage health.

Dose: 1500mg glucosamine / 1200mg chondroitin

Important Notes on Supplements

  • Supplements don't replace a good diet—they supplement it
  • Quality varies widely—choose reputable brands with third-party testing
  • Some supplements interact with medications or anesthesia
  • Tell your surgeon about all supplements before surgery
  • More is not always better—stick to recommended doses

Hydration

Adequate hydration supports every aspect of recovery—nutrient delivery, waste removal, joint lubrication, and tissue healing.

Hydration Guidelines

  • Baseline: 0.5-1 oz per pound of body weight daily
  • 150 lb person: 75-150 oz (about 2-4.5 liters)
  • More if: Hot weather, fever, sweating during PT
  • Signs of adequate hydration: Pale yellow urine

Best Hydration Sources

  • Water (primary source)
  • Herbal tea
  • Water-rich foods (cucumbers, watermelon, soups)
  • Electrolyte drinks during intense PT sessions

Medication Note

If you're taking pain medications (especially NSAIDs), adequate hydration is extra important to support kidney function.

Foods to Limit

While no foods are completely off-limits, some can promote inflammation or impair healing when consumed in excess.

Alcohol

Impairs wound healing, interacts with pain medications, promotes inflammation, and disrupts sleep. Avoid completely for at least the first few weeks, then limit significantly.

Added Sugars

Excess sugar promotes inflammation and can impair immune function. Limit sodas, candy, pastries, and sweetened drinks.

Processed Foods

Often high in omega-6 fats, sodium, and additives that may promote inflammation. Choose whole foods when possible.

Trans Fats

Found in some fried and processed foods. Strongly pro-inflammatory. Check labels for "partially hydrogenated oils."

Excessive Omega-6

While some omega-6 is needed, excess (from vegetable oils, fried foods) can promote inflammation. Balance with omega-3s.

Nutrition by Recovery Phase

Pre-Surgery (Prehab Phase)

  • Build up protein intake
  • Optimize vitamin D levels (get tested if possible)
  • Load up on anti-inflammatory foods
  • Stop certain supplements that affect bleeding (fish oil, vitamin E—ask surgeon)
  • Stay well-hydrated

Weeks 0-2 (Acute Healing)

  • Highest protein priority (1.6-2.0 g/kg)
  • Don't drastically cut calories
  • Focus on vitamin C, zinc, vitamin A for wound healing
  • Easy-to-digest foods if appetite is reduced
  • Fiber to counteract constipation from pain meds
  • Avoid alcohol completely

Weeks 2-6 (Early Rehab)

  • Continue high protein intake
  • Resume fish oil/omega-3 supplements
  • Consider collagen supplementation before PT
  • Anti-inflammatory foods to manage swelling
  • Calcium and vitamin D for bone healing

Months 2-6 (Strength Building)

  • Maintain high protein for muscle building
  • Adjust calories based on activity and goals
  • Creatine may support strength gains
  • Time protein around workouts
  • Continue anti-inflammatory focus

Months 6+ (Return to Sport)

  • Sport-specific nutrition timing
  • Performance-focused eating
  • Continue adequate protein
  • Hydration for training demands
  • Consider working with sports dietitian

Sample Meal Ideas

High-Protein Breakfast

  • 3-egg omelet with spinach and feta
  • Greek yogurt with berries and walnuts
  • Protein smoothie with collagen, fruit, and spinach

Recovery Lunch

  • Grilled salmon over mixed greens with olive oil
  • Chicken stir-fry with colorful vegetables and turmeric
  • Lentil soup with whole grain bread

Anti-Inflammatory Dinner

  • Baked salmon with roasted sweet potatoes and broccoli
  • Lean beef with quinoa and roasted vegetables
  • Chicken with turmeric rice and sautéed greens

Protein-Rich Snacks

  • Cottage cheese with fruit
  • Hard-boiled eggs
  • Protein shake
  • Greek yogurt
  • Jerky (low sodium)
  • Cheese with apple slices

Key Takeaways

Prioritize Protein

1.6-2.0 g/kg body weight, distributed across meals

Don't Under-Eat

Healing requires calories—this isn't the time for aggressive dieting

Eat Anti-Inflammatory

Omega-3s, colorful produce, herbs and spices

Key Nutrients

Vitamin C, D, calcium, zinc for healing

Stay Hydrated

0.5-1 oz per pound of body weight

Limit Inflammatory Foods

Alcohol, excess sugar, processed foods
