ACL Surgery Decisions

Everything you need to make informed decisions about ACL surgery: surgical vs. non-surgical treatment, graft options, costs, timing, and success rates.

ACL Surgery vs. No Surgery

Not everyone with an ACL tear needs surgery. The decision depends on your symptoms, activity goals, and individual factors.

Surgery is Typically Recommended If:

  • Your knee gives way during daily activities
  • You want to return to cutting/pivoting sports
  • You have associated injuries (meniscus tears)
  • You're young with many active years ahead
  • You're an athlete wanting to compete at high level
  • Conservative treatment hasn't resolved instability

Non-Surgical Treatment May Work If:

  • Your knee feels stable with daily activities
  • You're willing to avoid high-risk sports
  • You have low activity demands
  • You're older with less demanding lifestyle
  • No associated meniscus or cartilage injuries
  • You can commit to extensive physical therapy

Important Consideration

Living with ACL deficiency risks additional damage. Each giving-way episode can cause meniscus tears and cartilage damage, accelerating arthritis development.

Key Questions to Ask Yourself

1
Does my knee feel unstable during normal daily activities?

ACL Graft Types: Which is Best?

ACL reconstruction requires replacing the torn ligament with a tissue graft. The four main options each have advantages and disadvantages.

Patellar Tendon (BTB)

Center third of patellar tendon with bone plugs from kneecap and shin.

Pros: Strong bone-to-bone healing, excellent fixation, "gold standard" for high-level athletes

Cons: Anterior knee pain (15-30%), kneeling discomfort, donor site morbidity

Best for: High-level athletes, those prioritizing graft strength

Patellar Tendon Guide →

Hamstring Tendon

Semitendinosus and gracilis tendons from back of thigh.

Pros: Less anterior knee pain, smaller incision, less kneeling discomfort

Cons: Soft tissue-to-bone healing (slower), potential hamstring weakness (10-15%)

Best for: Most recreational athletes, those concerned about anterior knee pain

Hamstring Graft Guide →

Quadriceps Tendon

Central portion of quadriceps tendon above kneecap.

Pros: Thick strong graft, less hamstring weakness, versatile

Cons: Less long-term data, potential quad weakness, newer technique

Best for: Revision surgery, larger patients, those wanting to preserve hamstrings

Quad Tendon Guide →

Allograft (Donor Tissue)

Tissue from a cadaver donor (patellar, Achilles, tibialis anterior).

Pros: No donor site morbidity, smaller incisions, less initial pain

Cons: Higher re-tear rate in young athletes (2-4x), slower incorporation, disease transmission risk (very rare)

Best for: Older patients (40+), lower activity demands, revision surgery

Factors Affecting Success

  • Rehabilitation quality: Most important factor
  • Graft type: Autograft better than allograft in young patients
  • Return timing: Waiting 9+ months reduces re-tear
  • Meeting criteria: Strength and functional testing
  • Surgeon experience: High-volume surgeons have better outcomes

Re-tear Risk Factors

  • Age under 25 (highest risk group)
  • Return to sport before 9 months
  • Allograft use in young active patients
  • Not meeting return-to-sport criteria
  • Playing high-risk sports (soccer, basketball)
  • Inadequate rehabilitation

Cost-Saving Tips

  • Ambulatory surgery centers are often cheaper than hospitals
  • Ask about cash-pay discounts (often 20-40% off)
  • Get itemized estimates before surgery
  • Verify in-network status for all providers
  • Consider medical credit cards or payment plans
  • Check if your plan covers physical therapy visits

Hidden Costs to Consider

  • Pre-op visits and MRI
  • Post-op follow-up visits

  • Brace and crutches
  • Time off work
  • Home care assistance

Surgery Timing & Age Considerations

When to Have Surgery

Optimal Timing: 3-6 Weeks Post-Injury

Going into surgery with the following predicts better outcomes:

  • Minimal swelling
  • Full extension (knee straightening)
  • Good quad function
  • Flexion to at least 120 degrees

This is why prehab is so important!

Risks of Immediate Surgery

  • Higher risk of arthrofibrosis (scar tissue)
  • More difficult rehabilitation
  • Potentially worse outcomes

Risks of Long Delay (6+ months)

  • Additional meniscus damage
  • Cartilage degeneration
  • Muscle atrophy
  • More complex surgery

Age Considerations

No Strict Age Limit

ACL reconstruction can be successful at any adult age. Consider:

  • Under 25: Highest re-tear risk; autograft strongly recommended
  • 25-40: Lower re-tear risk; all graft options reasonable
  • Over 40: Good outcomes; allograft becomes more reasonable option
  • Over 50-60: Still beneficial if active and symptomatic

Surgery Over 40/50: What to Know

  • Outcomes are generally good
  • Lower re-tear rates (less high-risk activity)
  • Slightly higher complication rates
  • Decision based on activity goals and symptoms
  • May have longer recovery time

Age alone shouldn't prevent surgery if you're active and your knee is unstable.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is ACL surgery outpatient? +

Yes, ACL reconstruction is typically an outpatient procedure. Most patients go home the same day, within 2-4 hours after surgery. The procedure itself takes 60-90 minutes. You'll need someone to drive you home and stay with you for 24 hours. Rarely, overnight observation may be needed for pain management or medical complications.

What's the difference between ACL reconstruction and ACL repair? +

ACL Reconstruction: The torn ACL is removed and replaced with a graft (autograft or allograft). This is the standard of care for most ACL tears.

ACL Repair: The torn ACL is surgically reattached. This is only possible for certain tear patterns (proximal avulsions) and is less commonly performed. Some newer techniques are emerging but reconstruction remains the gold standard.

Can I live without an ACL? +

Yes, many people function well without an ACL. You can typically walk, bike, swim, and do linear activities. However, you'll need to avoid cutting, pivoting, and jumping sports. Some people ("copers") do fine; others experience frequent instability. Living with ACL deficiency risks additional damage from giving-way episodes. Success depends on muscle strength, activity modification, and your specific demands.

How do I choose a surgeon? +

Look for: board certification in orthopedic surgery with sports medicine fellowship training, high volume of ACL reconstructions (50+ per year ideal), experience with your preferred graft type, good communication and willingness to answer questions, and positive reviews from ACL patients specifically. Consider getting a second opinion. Ask about their specific outcomes data if available.

What if I delay ACL surgery for years? +

ACL reconstruction can still be performed years after injury, but risks include: accumulated meniscus damage, cartilage degeneration, more difficult surgery due to chronic changes, and potentially worse long-term outcomes. If your knee has been stable and you've avoided instability episodes, outcomes may still be good. Chronic ACL deficiency is associated with earlier arthritis development.
