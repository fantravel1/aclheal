ACL Prehab: Preparing for Surgery

What you do before surgery significantly impacts your recovery. Learn the exercises, strategies, and goals to optimize your outcomes.

Why Is Prehab Important?

Prehab (pre-surgery rehabilitation) significantly improves ACL surgical outcomes. Research shows patients who do prehab have:

  • Faster recovery — Better function at all post-op time points
  • Lower complication rates — Up to 50% reduction in arthrofibrosis
  • Better 2-year outcomes — Pre-op quad strength predicts long-term results
  • Improved mental readiness — Confidence going into surgery

Key insight: Going into surgery with a stiff, swollen knee with poor quad function predicts worse outcomes. Prehab addresses all of these.

Don't Skip Prehab

Patients who go into surgery with:

  • Limited extension (< 5 degrees) → Higher arthrofibrosis risk
  • Significant swelling → More post-op complications
  • Poor quad activation → Slower strength recovery

Your surgeon may delay surgery if you haven't met prehab goals.

Prehab Goals Before Surgery

📏

Full Extension

0 degrees or matching other knee

💪

Good Quad Activation

Ability to fire quad and do SLR

💧

Minimal Swelling

Little to no visible effusion

🚶

Normal Gait

Walking without limp

Range of Motion Targets

  • Extension: 0 degrees (full straight) — MOST IMPORTANT
  • Flexion: ≥120 degrees (ideally 130+)

If you can't achieve full extension, your surgeon may delay surgery. Missing extension before surgery significantly increases arthrofibrosis risk.

Strength Targets

  • Straight leg raise without extension lag
  • Good quad firing with quad sets
  • Ability to walk without significant limp

Essential Prehab Exercises

Quad Sets

The most important prehab exercise. Tighten your quadriceps muscle by pressing the back of your knee down into the floor/bed. Hold for 5-10 seconds.

Sets/Reps: 10 reps × 10 sets daily Focus: Quad activation

Straight Leg Raises (SLR)

Lock your knee straight, then lift your leg 6-12 inches off the surface. Keep the knee fully extended throughout. Lower slowly.

Sets/Reps: 3 sets × 10-15 reps Focus: Quad strength

Detailed SLR instructions →

Heel Slides

Lying on your back, slide your heel toward your buttock by bending your knee. Use a strap or towel to assist if needed. Slide back out slowly.

Sets/Reps: 3 sets × 15-20 reps Focus: Flexion ROM

Detailed heel slide instructions →

Prone Hangs / Extension Stretching

Lie face down with your knee cap just off the edge of the bed. Let gravity straighten your knee. Can add light ankle weight for more stretch.

Duration: 10-15 minutes × 2-3 times daily Focus: Extension ROM (CRITICAL)

Ankle Pumps

Pump your ankle up and down repeatedly. This promotes blood circulation and helps reduce swelling.

Sets/Reps: 20-30 reps × multiple times daily Focus: Circulation, swelling reduction

Stationary Cycling

Once ROM allows, gentle cycling helps maintain fitness and improves ROM. Start with high seat, low resistance, rocking motion if needed.

Duration: 10-20 minutes daily Focus: ROM, cardiovascular fitness

How to Reduce Swelling Before Surgery

RICE Protocol

  • Rest: Avoid activities that aggravate swelling
  • Ice: 20 minutes on, 40 minutes off, multiple times daily
  • Compression: Compression sleeve or ACE wrap
  • Elevation: Keep leg elevated above heart when resting

Additional Strategies

  • Ankle pumps: Promote venous return
  • Gentle movement: ROM exercises within pain limits
  • Anti-inflammatories: If approved by surgeon (NSAIDs)
  • Aquatic therapy: Water pressure naturally reduces swelling
  • Avoid prolonged standing/walking

Daily Swelling Management Checklist

✓ Ice 3-4 times for 20 minutes
✓ Wear compression sleeve during day
✓ Elevate leg when sitting/lying
✓ Ankle pumps every hour when awake
✓ Limit prolonged standing
✓ Complete ROM exercises

Prehab Timeline

Most patients need 2-6 weeks of prehab. Progress is goal-based, not time-based.

Immediately After Injury

Focus on swelling control (RICE), protected weight bearing, and gentle ROM exercises. Begin quad sets as soon as tolerable. See a physical therapist or your surgeon.

Week 1-2

Continue swelling management. Work on regaining full extension (prone hangs, heel props). Progress quad sets and begin SLRs. Heel slides for flexion. Goal: Walking with minimal limp.

Week 2-4

Achieve full extension. Continue strengthening (SLRs, mini squats, step-ups). Add stationary bike if ROM allows. Normalize gait pattern. Reduce swelling to minimal.

Week 4-6

Maintain gains. Continue strengthening. Mental preparation for surgery. Ensure all prehab goals are met. Schedule surgery when criteria achieved.

Preparing for Surgery Day

Before Surgery Checklist

✓ Complete all pre-op appointments and testing
✓ Fill prescriptions (pain meds, blood thinners)
✓ Arrange ride home and 24-hour helper
✓ Set up recovery area at home
✓ Stock up on ice packs and ice machine
✓ Prepare easy meals and snacks
✓ Get crutches and learn to use them
✓ Arrange time off work

Home Setup Tips

  • Create a recovery station (couch/recliner with everything within reach)
  • Put commonly used items at counter height
  • Remove trip hazards (rugs, cords)
  • Consider a shower chair or bath bench
  • Have a cooler nearby for ice packs
  • Charge devices and set up entertainment

Night Before Surgery

  • No food after midnight (or as directed)
  • Shower with antibacterial soap
  • Don't shave your knee area
  • Wear loose, comfortable clothing
  • Leave valuables at home
  • Get a good night's sleep

Frequently Asked Questions

How long should I do prehab before ACL surgery? +

Most surgeons recommend 2-6 weeks of prehab, but duration depends on meeting goals rather than a fixed timeline. You should achieve full extension, minimal swelling, good quad activation, and flexion ≥120 degrees. Some patients achieve this quickly; others need longer. Don't rush to surgery before you're ready.

Can I do too much prehab? +

It's possible to overdo it and cause increased swelling or irritation. Signs you're doing too much: swelling increases after exercise, pain worsens, or you feel exhausted. Listen to your body and communicate with your PT. The goal is to reduce swelling and restore function, not to get a workout. Quality over quantity.

What if I can't achieve full extension? +

This is the most important prehab goal. If you're struggling, work closely with a physical therapist who can use manual techniques. Increase frequency of prone hangs and extension stretches. Your surgeon may delay surgery until extension is achieved. Going into surgery without full extension significantly increases complications.

Should I wear my brace during prehab? +

Follow your surgeon's guidance. Many patients wear a brace for activities that might stress the knee but remove it for exercises. As swelling decreases and stability improves, you may use it less. Don't become dependent on the brace—you want to build muscle strength. Remove it for ROM exercises.
