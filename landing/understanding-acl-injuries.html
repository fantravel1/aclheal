Understanding ACL Injuries

Everything you need to know about ACL tears: what they are, how they happen, symptoms to watch for, and the different grades of injury. Expert answers to your most pressing questions.

What is an ACL Tear?

The ACL (Anterior Cruciate Ligament) is one of four major ligaments in your knee. It connects your thigh bone (femur) to your shin bone (tibia) and provides crucial stability, especially during:

  • Pivoting and changing direction
  • Jumping and landing
  • Sudden stops and starts
  • Rotational movements

An ACL tear occurs when this ligament is stretched beyond its capacity or completely ruptured. About 70-80% of ACL tears are non-contact injuries—meaning they happen without another person hitting your knee.

Common Causes

  • Sudden pivoting or cutting movements
  • Landing awkwardly from a jump
  • Stopping suddenly while running
  • Direct collision or tackle
  • Hyperextension of the knee

ACL Tear Symptoms: How Do I Know If I Tore My ACL?

Recognizing ACL tear symptoms can help you seek appropriate treatment quickly. Here are the key signs:

Classic ACL Tear Signs

  • 🔊
    Audible "pop" — About 70% of people hear or feel a pop at the moment of injury
  • 💧
    Rapid swelling — Significant swelling within 2-6 hours (hemarthrosis - bleeding in the joint)
  • ⚠
    Severe pain — Immediate pain, especially when trying to continue activity
  • 💪
    Instability — Knee feels like it will "give way" or buckle
  • 🚫
    Difficulty bearing weight — Unable to walk normally or continue playing
  • 🔄
    Loss of range of motion — Difficulty fully straightening or bending the knee

"I Heard a Pop in My Knee"

If you heard a pop during a knee injury, it's a significant indicator but not definitive proof of an ACL tear. Here's what to consider:

  • Pop + rapid swelling + instability = very likely ACL tear
  • Other structures (meniscus, other ligaments) can also cause a pop
  • Some ACL tears occur without a noticeable pop

Bottom line: If you heard a pop and have significant symptoms, see an orthopedic specialist for proper evaluation.

Can You Have an ACL Tear Without Swelling?

While uncommon, ACL tears can present with minimal swelling in cases of:

  • Partial tears with less bleeding
  • Chronic or old tears
  • Tears where blood drains from the joint capsule

Don't rule out an ACL tear based solely on lack of swelling.

ACL Tear Grades: Grade 1, 2, and 3 Explained

ACL injuries are classified into three grades based on severity:

GRADE 1

Mild Sprain

Ligament stretched but intact

  • Knee remains stable
  • Mild pain and swelling
  • Usually heals with rest and PT
  • Recovery: 2-4 weeks
GRADE 2

Partial Tear

Ligament partially torn

  • Some instability possible
  • Moderate pain and swelling
  • May heal without surgery
  • Recovery: varies widely
GRADE 3

Complete Tear

Ligament fully ruptured

  • Significant instability
  • Severe initial pain/swelling
  • Usually requires surgery
  • Recovery: 9-12 months

Partial vs. Complete ACL Tear

The distinction matters for treatment planning:

Aspect Partial Tear Complete Tear
Ligament Status Some fibers intact, some stability remains No connection between ends
Stability May feel stable for daily activities Often unstable, especially with pivoting
Healing Potential May improve with conservative treatment Cannot heal on its own
Surgery Sometimes avoidable Usually needed for active individuals

Can You Walk on a Torn ACL?

Yes, many people can walk on a completely torn ACL once the initial pain and swelling subside (usually 2-4 weeks after injury).

This is because walking in a straight line doesn't require ACL function. The ACL primarily provides:

  • Rotational stability
  • Prevention of anterior tibial translation (shin sliding forward)
  • Stability during cutting and pivoting

Important: Being able to walk doesn't mean your ACL is intact or that you don't need treatment. Many people with complete ACL tears can walk normally but experience problems with:

  • Unexpected giving way episodes
  • Difficulty with stairs or uneven terrain
  • Inability to return to cutting/pivoting sports

Walking Ability ≠ Injury Severity

You can have a complete ACL tear and:

  • Walk without a limp
  • Climb stairs
  • Ride a bike
  • Swim

But you may still need surgery if you want to return to sports involving cutting, pivoting, or jumping.

When Does Walking Become Difficult?

  • First 48-72 hours: Difficult due to pain/swelling
  • Week 1-2: Possible with crutches
  • Week 2-4: Often can walk without aids
  • Long-term: Normal walking usually possible

ACL Injury vs. Sprain vs. Strain: What's the Difference?

Term Definition Applies to ACL?
ACL Injury General term for any damage to the ACL Yes - umbrella term
ACL Sprain Stretching or tearing of a ligament (connects bone to bone) Yes - all ACL tears are technically sprains
ACL Strain Stretching or tearing of a muscle or tendon (connects muscle to bone) No - technically incorrect term for ACL

The Correct Terminology

Medically speaking:

  • Sprains affect ligaments (like the ACL, MCL, LCL, PCL)
  • Strains affect muscles and tendons (like hamstring strains)

So technically, you can't "strain" your ACL—it would be a sprain. However, in everyday language, people often use these terms interchangeably. When communicating with your doctor, the specific grade (1, 2, or 3) matters more than whether you call it a sprain or strain.

ACL vs. MCL Tears: What's the Difference?

Characteristic ACL (Anterior Cruciate Ligament) MCL (Medial Collateral Ligament)
Location Inside the knee joint (central) Inner (medial) side of knee
Function Prevents forward sliding and rotation Prevents sideways (valgus) bending
Common Injury Mechanism Non-contact pivoting, landing Direct blow to outer knee
Healing Potential Poor - usually doesn't heal Good - often heals without surgery
Surgery Required? Usually for active individuals Rarely (Grade 3 sometimes)
Recovery Time 9-12 months post-surgery 4-8 weeks for most grades

Combined ACL + MCL Injuries

ACL and MCL injuries can occur together, especially from significant trauma. Treatment approach may differ—often the MCL is allowed to heal first before ACL reconstruction, or both may be addressed simultaneously depending on severity.

Why Are ACL Injuries So Common in Women?

2-8x Higher ACL injury rate in female athletes vs. males in the same sports

Multiple factors contribute to higher ACL injury rates in female athletes:

Anatomical Factors

  • Wider pelvis — Increases the Q-angle (angle from hip to knee)
  • Narrower intercondylar notch — Less space for the ACL
  • Smaller ACL — May be weaker relative to body size
  • Greater ligament laxity — More joint looseness on average

Hormonal Factors

  • Estrogen may affect ligament strength and laxity
  • Some studies show increased risk during certain menstrual cycle phases
  • Research is ongoing and not fully conclusive

Biomechanical Factors

  • Landing mechanics — Women tend to land with less knee flexion
  • Knee valgus — Greater tendency for "knock-knee" position
  • Hip control — Differences in hip muscle activation

Prevention Programs Work

Neuromuscular training programs like FIFA 11+ and the PEP Program can reduce ACL injury rates in female athletes by 50-70%.

Frequently Asked Questions

How long after an ACL tear can you still get surgery? +

There's no strict deadline for ACL surgery. Some people have successful reconstruction years after their original injury. However, delaying surgery while continuing activities that cause instability can lead to additional damage (meniscus tears, cartilage damage). Most surgeons recommend reconstruction within 3-6 months for active individuals. Chronic ACL deficiency is associated with higher rates of osteoarthritis.

Can an ACL tear heal on its own? +

A completely torn ACL cannot heal or regenerate on its own. The ACL has poor blood supply and is surrounded by synovial fluid, which prevents healing. However, this doesn't mean everyone needs surgery. Non-surgical management (physical therapy, bracing, activity modification) can work well for people with stable knees who don't participate in high-risk activities. Some partial tears may improve with conservative treatment.

What does a torn ACL feel like? +

At the moment of injury, many people report hearing or feeling a "pop." This is followed by immediate pain that can range from moderate to severe. Within hours, significant swelling typically develops. After the acute phase, the knee may feel unstable or "loose," especially during twisting or pivoting movements. Some describe it as feeling like the knee will buckle or give way. Daily activities like walking may feel relatively normal, but cutting or pivoting feels unreliable.

How is an ACL tear diagnosed? +

ACL tears are diagnosed through a combination of physical examination and imaging. An experienced physician can often diagnose an ACL tear through clinical tests like the Lachman test, anterior drawer test, and pivot shift test. MRI is the gold standard imaging study, with 90-95% accuracy for ACL tears. X-rays may be taken to rule out fractures but cannot show the ACL itself.

What sports have the highest ACL injury rates? +

Sports with the highest ACL injury rates involve cutting, pivoting, and jumping. The highest risk sports include: soccer (football), basketball, skiing, volleyball, gymnastics, and lacrosse. Female athletes in these sports have particularly high rates. Contact football has high overall knee injury rates but ACL tears specifically are often non-contact. Sports like swimming, cycling, and running have very low ACL injury rates.
