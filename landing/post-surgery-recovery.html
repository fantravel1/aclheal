ACL Recovery Timeline: Week by Week

Days 1-3: The Hardest Days

What to expect: This is typically the most challenging period. Pain peaks as nerve blocks wear off (usually day 2). Significant swelling is normal. Fatigue from anesthesia.

Focus on: Pain management (take medications as prescribed), icing every 1-2 hours, elevation above heart level, basic exercises (ankle pumps, quad sets), staying ahead of pain.

Detailed Week 0-2 Guide →

Week 1: Foundation Building

Goals: Control swelling, manage pain, achieve full extension, begin quad activation.

Exercises: Quad sets (10×10 daily), ankle pumps, heel slides, passive extension stretches.

Activity: Walking with crutches, weight-bearing as tolerated, first PT visit.

Normal: Significant swelling, difficulty sleeping, reliance on pain medication.

Week 2: Gaining Mobility

Goals: Maintain full extension, improve flexion (goal: 90+ degrees), reduce swelling.

Exercises: Continue week 1 exercises, add straight leg raises, short arc quads.

Activity: Increase walking distance, begin weaning from strongest pain meds.

Normal: Still significant swelling, sleep improving, incision healing.

Weeks 3-4: Crutch Weaning

Goals: Full extension, flexion 100-110°, walk without limp, wean from crutches.

Exercises: Progressive strengthening, balance work, stationary bike (if ROM allows).

Activity: Transition to one crutch, then none as quad control improves.

Milestones: Off crutches (most patients), off opioid medications.

Weeks 5-6: Building Strength

Goals: Flexion 120+°, good quad control, normalize gait.

Exercises: Closed chain strengthening (mini squats, step-ups), bike with full revolutions.

Activity: Walking longer distances, may return to driving (right leg: ~6 weeks).

Normal: Ongoing swelling after activity, some stiffness.

Pain Management After ACL Surgery

Pain Timeline

  • Day 0: Minimal pain (nerve block working)
  • Days 2-4: Peak pain as nerve block wears off
  • Week 1: Pain managed with medications
  • Week 2: Transitioning to OTC medications
  • Weeks 3+: Manageable with ice and activity modification

Pain Management Strategies

  • Stay ahead of pain: Take medications on schedule, not just when hurting
  • Ice frequently: 20 minutes on, 40+ minutes off
  • Elevate: Above heart level to reduce swelling
  • Move gently: Motion helps prevent stiffness
  • Multimodal approach: Ice + medication + elevation works better than any alone

Medication Safety

  • Take opioids only as prescribed
  • Never drive or make important decisions on opioids
  • Wean off opioids as soon as possible (typically by week 2)
  • Don't mix alcohol with pain medications
  • Ask about stool softeners (opioids cause constipation)

When to Call Your Surgeon

  • Pain suddenly worsening after improving
  • Fever over 101.5°F (38.6°C)
  • Increasing redness or warmth at incision
  • New numbness or tingling
  • Calf pain or swelling (possible blood clot)
  • Pain not controlled by medications

ACL Surgery Swelling Timeline

Normal Swelling Timeline

  • Week 1-2: Significant swelling—this is expected
  • Week 3-6: Gradual decrease but still present
  • Week 6-12: Continues to improve, may increase with activity
  • Month 3-6: Mild intermittent swelling normal
  • Month 6-12: Most swelling resolves

Key insight: Swelling at 6 weeks is very common and not concerning unless accompanied by other symptoms.

Swelling Management

  • Ice: Multiple times daily, especially after activity
  • Compression: Sleeve or wrap during day
  • Elevation: Above heart level when resting
  • Activity modification: Rest if swelling increases
  • Ankle pumps: Promote circulation

Daily Life After ACL Surgery

Sleeping After ACL Surgery

Best position: On your back with leg elevated on pillows.

  • Keep knee straight, not bent
  • Wear brace if instructed
  • Side sleeping OK after 2-3 weeks with pillow between knees
  • Avoid sleeping on stomach
  • Recliner can be more comfortable initially

Showering After Surgery

  • Follow surgeon's specific instructions
  • Keep incisions dry until cleared (usually 2-14 days)
  • Use waterproof covering or sponge bath initially
  • No baths or pools until fully healed (2-4 weeks)
  • Consider shower chair for safety

Returning to Work

  • Desk/work from home: 1-2 weeks (can elevate leg)
  • Office with commute: 2-4 weeks
  • Light physical work: 4-6 weeks
  • Moderate physical: 3-4 months
  • Heavy labor: 6-9+ months

Consider: ability to elevate, transportation, PT schedule, pain medication use.

Driving Timeline

  • Left leg (automatic): 1-2 weeks
  • Right leg: 4-6 weeks
  • Requirements: Off opioids, adequate reaction time, surgeon clearance

Frequently Asked Questions

Why is my knee still stiff months after surgery? +

Some stiffness in the first 3-6 months is common. Causes include: scar tissue formation (normal healing process), swelling limiting motion, muscle tightness and weakness, and not doing enough ROM exercises. Concerning stiffness: inability to achieve full extension, flexion significantly less than other knee, or motion getting worse over time. If stiffness isn't improving with PT, discuss with your surgeon—sometimes additional intervention (manipulation or arthroscopy) is needed.

Is it normal to have clicking or popping after ACL surgery? +

Some clicking or popping is common and usually benign. Causes: scar tissue breaking up, fluid in the joint, hardware (screws/buttons), and normal joint sounds. Concerning clicks: painful clicking, catching or locking, or clicking with giving way. Painless clicking that isn't limiting your function is typically not worrisome. Mention to your surgeon at follow-up appointments.

When can I stop wearing my brace? +

Brace protocols vary by surgeon. Common approaches: Weeks 0-2: Brace locked in extension for sleeping, walking. Weeks 2-6: Brace for walking, off for exercises and home. Weeks 6+: May discontinue for most activities. Some surgeons don't use braces at all post-operatively. Follow your surgeon's specific instructions. The brace primarily provides protection and confidence in early recovery.

How much should I ice my knee? +

First 2 weeks: Ice as frequently as possible—20 minutes on, 40+ minutes off. Some use ice machines that allow more continuous icing. After 2 weeks: Continue icing after exercises and as needed for swelling. After 6 weeks: Ice after more demanding activities or when swelling increases. Long-term: Many patients continue icing after PT sessions for months. Ice is your friend throughout recovery.

When will my knee feel normal again? +

This varies significantly. Many patients report: 3 months: Better, but still aware of the knee. 6 months: Good for daily activities, but not "normal." 9-12 months: Close to normal for most activities. 18-24 months: When many feel "fully recovered." Some residual awareness or occasional symptoms may persist. Complete "forgetting" about the knee may take 2+ years for some. Focus on function rather than timeline.
