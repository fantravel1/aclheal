ACL Surgery Complications & Concerns

Understanding potential complications, warning signs to watch for, and when to seek medical attention during your ACL recovery.

Seek Immediate Medical Attention If You Experience:

  • Fever over 101.5°F (38.6°C)
  • Increasing redness, warmth, or pus at incision sites
  • Sudden severe calf pain or swelling (possible blood clot)
  • Chest pain or difficulty breathing
  • Sudden giving way with new swelling (possible re-tear)
  • Severe pain not controlled by medications

Potential Complications After ACL Surgery

ACL Graft Failure / Re-tear

Rate: 6-15% overall; higher in young athletes and with allograft

Signs of graft failure:

  • New giving-way episode, especially during cutting/pivoting
  • Hearing/feeling a pop similar to original injury
  • Rapid swelling after a specific incident
  • Loss of confidence in knee stability

Risk factors: Early return to sport (<9 months), allograft in young patients, high-risk activities, inadequate rehabilitation.

What to do: See your surgeon immediately for evaluation. MRI can confirm diagnosis. Revision surgery may be needed.

Arthrofibrosis (Excessive Scar Tissue)

Rate: 4-12%

Symptoms:

  • Inability to fully straighten the knee (loss of extension)
  • Significantly limited bending (flexion)
  • Persistent stiffness despite physical therapy
  • Feeling of knee being "stuck"

Risk factors: Going into surgery with limited ROM, inadequate early rehab, prolonged immobilization, individual healing response.

Treatment: Aggressive PT, manipulation under anesthesia, or arthroscopic lysis (surgical removal).

Prevention: Prehab to ensure full extension before surgery; prioritize extension exercises post-op.

Cyclops Lesion

Rate: 1-10%

What it is: A nodule of scar tissue at the front of the graft that blocks full extension.

Symptoms:

  • Loss of full extension (had it, then lost it)
  • Painful clicking or clunking with straightening
  • Feeling of mechanical block

Treatment: Arthroscopic surgery to remove the lesion. Usually very successful.

Infection

Rate: <1%

Signs: Fever, increasing redness/warmth at incision, pus, worsening pain, foul smell.

Action: Contact surgeon immediately. May require antibiotics or surgical washout.

Blood Clots (DVT)

Rate: <1%

Signs: Calf pain, swelling, warmth in lower leg, redness.

Action: Seek immediate medical attention. Blood thinners may be needed.

Prevention: Ankle pumps, early mobilization, compression.

Nerve Damage

Rate: 1-5% (usually temporary)

Symptoms: Numbness near incision (very common and usually resolves), tingling, weakness (rare).

Notes: Small patch of numbness below incision is common and often permanent but not problematic.

Persistent Pain

Rate: varies

Causes: Patellar tendonitis (BTB graft), neuroma, hardware irritation, patellofemoral issues.

Action: Discuss with surgeon. May require hardware removal, PT, or further evaluation.

Common Concerns (Usually Normal)

Clicking/Popping After Surgery

Usually normal if:

  • Painless
  • Not associated with instability
  • Doesn't limit function

Common causes: Scar tissue breaking up, fluid in joint, hardware (screws/buttons), normal joint sounds.

Concerning if: Painful, associated with catching/locking, or causes giving way.

Persistent Swelling

Usually normal if:

  • Mild and intermittent
  • Related to activity level
  • Responds to ice and rest
  • Gradually improving over months

Concerning if: Sudden onset, associated with warmth/redness, fever, not responding to rest.

Some swelling at 6 months is very common.

ACL Re-tear Rates: The Statistics

Overall Re-tear Risk

  • Overall: 6-15%
  • Same knee: 6-8%
  • Opposite knee: 8-12%

Higher Risk Groups

  • Age <25: 15-25% combined risk (either knee)
  • Allograft in young athletes: up to 25%
  • Return <9 months: significantly higher
  • High-risk sports: soccer, basketball

How to Reduce Risk

  • Wait at least 9 months before return to sport
  • Meet all return-to-sport criteria (strength, hop tests)
  • Complete comprehensive rehabilitation
  • Continue prevention exercises after return
  • Choose autograft if young and active
  • Address psychological readiness

Frequently Asked Questions

Why does my knee still give out after ACL surgery? +

Several potential causes: 1) Graft failure—see your surgeon urgently to rule this out. 2) Muscle weakness—quad weakness is the most common cause of instability feeling even with intact graft. 3) Proprioceptive deficits—the knee hasn't relearned position sense. 4) Other injuries—meniscus or cartilage damage. 5) Psychological—fear causing hesitation. Get evaluated to confirm graft integrity, then focus on strengthening and neuromuscular training.

Is numbness around my incision normal? +

Yes, numbness around incision sites is very common after ACL surgery. Small sensory nerves are often cut during the procedure. A small patch of numbness, usually on the outer shin below the knee, is typical and often permanent but rarely problematic. Sensation may gradually improve over 6-12 months but may not fully return. This is usually just a cosmetic issue and doesn't affect function.

When should I worry about swelling? +

Worry about swelling if: it's sudden onset (not related to increased activity), associated with warmth or redness, accompanied by fever, not responding to ice/rest/elevation, worsening rather than improving over time, or accompanied by severe pain. Normal post-op swelling is gradual, activity-related, and responds to ice. If in doubt, contact your surgeon.

Can my hardware cause problems? +

Most people don't have issues with hardware (screws, buttons, interference screws), but some do experience: pain over prominent hardware, irritation with certain activities (kneeling), or psychological discomfort knowing it's there. Hardware can be removed after the graft has fully incorporated (usually 1-2 years post-surgery) if it's causing problems. This is a relatively minor procedure but usually not necessary.
