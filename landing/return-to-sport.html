Return to Sport Criteria

Meeting objective criteria before returning significantly reduces re-injury risk.

Category Test/Measure Target
Time Months post-surgery ≥9 months (some recommend 12)
Strength Quad Limb Symmetry Index (LSI) ≥90%
Strength Hamstring Limb Symmetry Index ≥90%
Functional Single Hop for Distance ≥90% symmetry
Functional Triple Hop for Distance ≥90% symmetry
Functional Crossover Hop for Distance ≥90% symmetry
Functional 6-meter Timed Hop ≥90% symmetry
Psychological ACL-RSI Score ≥65 (ideally >80)
Clinical Full ROM, no swelling, stable knee Normal exam
Training Sport-specific program completion All phases completed

Why Criteria Matter More Than Time

Time alone doesn't predict readiness. Two athletes at 9 months can have vastly different strength and function. Research shows athletes who pass return-to-sport testing have lower re-injury rates. Criteria-based progression is safer than time-based.

Overcoming Fear of Re-injury

Fear of re-injury is the #1 reason athletes don't return to sport—not physical limitations. 76% cite fear as their primary barrier.

Evidence-Based Strategies

  1. Graduated exposure: Progressively return to challenging movements
  2. Visualization: Mentally rehearse successful performance
  3. Build mastery: Meeting milestones proves your knee is ready
  4. Sports psychology: Work with mental performance specialist
  5. Peer support: Connect with athletes who've returned
  6. Focus on controllables: Preparation, strength, technique
  7. Accept uncertainty: Risk exists but you've minimized it

ACL-RSI: Measure Your Readiness

The ACL Return to Sport after Injury (ACL-RSI) scale measures psychological readiness. It assesses emotions, confidence, and risk appraisal.

  • Score >80: Good psychological readiness
  • Score 65-80: Moderate readiness, address specific concerns
  • Score <65: Low readiness, focus on psychological prep

When to Seek Help

Consider sports psychology if:

  • Fear is disproportionate to your physical readiness
  • You avoid drills your knee can handle
  • Anxiety is affecting sleep or mood
  • ACL-RSI score remains low despite progress

Sport-Specific Return Timelines

Soccer

Typical return: 9-12 months | Risk level: High

Soccer has one of the highest ACL injury and re-injury rates. Requires cutting, pivoting, and contact. Many professionals take 10-12 months. Continue FIFA 11+ prevention program after return.

Basketball

Typical return: 9-12 months | Risk level: High

Involves jumping, cutting, pivoting, and contact. Focus on landing mechanics and lateral movements. Many NBA players take 10-12+ months.

Skiing

Typical return: 9-12 months | Risk level: Moderate-High

Unique challenges: cold affecting knee, variable terrain, falls. Often return for following season. Bracing more common in skiing.

Running

Typical return: 3-6 months | Risk level: Low

Straight-line running has low ACL re-injury risk. Start walk-jog at 12-16 weeks. Full running typically possible by 4-6 months. Trail running with terrain variability requires more caution.

Should You Wear a Brace?

The Evidence

Research shows: Functional braces do NOT significantly reduce ACL re-tear risk. They may provide proprioceptive feedback and psychological confidence. They do not replace proper rehabilitation and strength.

Consider Wearing a Brace If:

  • It improves your confidence
  • Your sport allows it
  • Your surgeon recommends it
  • You're in early return phase (first season)

Consider Skipping If:

  • It restricts movement or performance
  • It gives false sense of security
  • It causes skin irritation
  • Your sport prohibits it

The Bottom Line

Bracing is a personal choice. Many athletes wear one for their first season back, then discontinue. Don't rely on a brace instead of meeting return criteria. Your strength and neuromuscular control provide far more protection than any brace.

Frequently Asked Questions

What sports have the highest ACL re-tear risk? +

Sports with highest re-tear risk: soccer, basketball, volleyball, skiing, lacrosse, handball, and football. These involve cutting, pivoting, jumping, and contact. Risk factors include unpredictable opponents, playing while fatigued, and compromised landing positions. Lower risk: running, cycling, swimming, rowing. Risk can be reduced by meeting criteria, prevention exercises, and avoiding fatigue.

Should I change sports after ACL surgery? +

This is a personal decision. Consider: your age and career stage, how important this specific sport is to you, your willingness to accept re-injury risk, and whether you can be happy with modified participation. Some athletes return to the same sport successfully; others choose lower-risk activities. There's no wrong answer—it depends on your values and risk tolerance.

How do I know if I'm truly ready to return? +

True readiness combines objective criteria (strength tests, hop tests, time) with subjective readiness (confidence, comfort). Ask yourself: Do I trust my knee? Can I move without thinking about it? Am I excited rather than terrified? Have I completed sport-specific training without issues? If you have doubts, continue training until you feel ready. Rushing rarely works out well.

What if I never want to return to sports? +

That's completely valid. Not everyone needs or wants to return to competitive sports. You can live an active, fulfilling life with recreational activities. Focus on activities you enjoy and that your knee tolerates. Many people find new activities they love post-ACL. The goal is a functional, pain-free knee for whatever life you want to live.
