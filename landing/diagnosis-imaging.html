ACL Tear Diagnosis & Imaging

Understanding how ACL tears are diagnosed through MRI, physical examination tests, and what to expect from your results.

How Are ACL Tears Diagnosed?

ACL tear diagnosis typically involves a combination of:

  1. Patient history — How the injury occurred, symptoms experienced
  2. Physical examination — Clinical tests to assess knee stability
  3. Imaging studies — MRI to confirm diagnosis and assess associated injuries

An experienced orthopedic surgeon can often diagnose an ACL tear through physical examination alone, with MRI used to confirm and evaluate for additional injuries like meniscus tears.

90-95% MRI accuracy for diagnosing ACL tears

Physical Examination Tests

Several clinical tests assess ACL integrity. These can be performed in the office without imaging.

Lachman Test 85-95% Sensitive

The most accurate clinical test for ACL tears.

How it's performed: With the knee bent at 20-30 degrees, the examiner stabilizes your thigh and pulls your shin forward.

Positive test: Increased forward movement with a soft or absent endpoint suggests ACL tear.

Why it's best: At this angle, other structures don't mask ACL laxity.

Anterior Drawer Test 62-70% Sensitive

Classic ACL test, but less sensitive than Lachman.

How it's performed: With knee bent at 90 degrees and foot flat, the examiner pulls the shin forward.

Positive test: Increased forward translation compared to the other knee.

Limitation: Hamstring tension at 90 degrees can mask ACL laxity.

Pivot Shift Test 98% Specific

Best predictor of functional instability.

How it's performed: The examiner extends the knee while applying rotation and valgus stress. If ACL-deficient, the tibia subluxates then reduces with a "clunk."

Positive test: Palpable or visible shift/clunk as the knee is flexed.

Limitation: Difficult to perform if patient is guarding due to pain.

Clinical Diagnosis Is Often Sufficient

In the hands of an experienced examiner, clinical tests can diagnose ACL tears without MRI. However, MRI is valuable for:

  • Confirming the diagnosis
  • Detecting meniscus tears (common with ACL injuries)
  • Identifying other ligament damage
  • Surgical planning

MRI for ACL Tears

What Does a Torn ACL Look Like on MRI?

MRI uses magnetic fields to create detailed images of soft tissues. A radiologist looks for specific signs of ACL injury:

⚠
Discontinuity

Gap in the ligament fibers

☀
Abnormal Signal

Ligament appears bright/white

→
Abnormal Orientation

Horizontal instead of diagonal

💧
Associated Findings

Bone bruising, effusion

Complete vs. Partial Tears on MRI

  • Complete tear: No visible intact fibers, often retracted ends
  • Partial tear: Some intact fibers visible, surrounding edema

What to Expect During MRI

  • Duration: 30-60 minutes
  • Position: Lying on your back with knee straight or slightly bent
  • Sensation: Loud clicking/banging sounds (earplugs provided)
  • Movement: Must stay still for clear images
  • Contrast: Usually not needed for ACL evaluation

Can an X-ray Show an ACL Tear?

No, X-rays cannot directly show ACL tears. X-rays only image bones, not soft tissues like ligaments, tendons, or cartilage.

Why X-rays Are Still Ordered

Despite not showing the ACL, X-rays are often part of knee injury evaluation to:

  • Rule out bone fractures (common with significant knee trauma)
  • Look for indirect signs of ACL injury
  • Assess joint alignment
  • Check for arthritis or other bone conditions

Indirect X-ray Signs of ACL Tear

  • Segond fracture: Small bone avulsion from lateral tibia—highly specific for ACL tear
  • Deep lateral femoral notch sign: Depression on the lateral femoral condyle
  • Joint effusion: Visible swelling in the joint
  • Tibial eminence fracture: Where the ACL attaches (common in children)

ACL Tear Diagnosis Without MRI

Yes, ACL tears can often be diagnosed without MRI. Experienced orthopedic surgeons have high accuracy with clinical examination alone.

When MRI May Not Be Necessary

  • Clear clinical exam findings (positive Lachman, pivot shift)
  • Classic injury mechanism and symptoms
  • Patient has already decided on surgery
  • Limited access to MRI (some countries/settings)

When MRI Is Recommended

  • Uncertain clinical findings
  • Need to assess for meniscus tears
  • Evaluating for other ligament injuries
  • Insurance or documentation requirements
  • Surgical planning purposes

The Bottom Line

MRI is helpful but not always essential. Trust your orthopedic surgeon's clinical judgment. If they're confident in the diagnosis from examination, MRI may be confirmatory rather than diagnostic.

Frequently Asked Questions

How much does an MRI for ACL cost? +

MRI costs vary significantly: With insurance, expect $100-500 out of pocket depending on your plan. Without insurance, MRIs can range from $500-3,000+. Imaging centers are often cheaper than hospital-based MRI. Some areas have cash-pay imaging centers offering MRIs for $300-500. Always verify coverage with your insurance before scheduling.

Can I get an MRI right after injury? +

Yes, but timing considerations exist. Immediate MRI is sometimes performed in emergency settings. However, some physicians prefer waiting 1-2 weeks for acute swelling to decrease, which can improve image quality. Bone bruising is actually more visible immediately after injury. Discuss timing with your physician based on your specific situation.

What if my MRI is negative but I still have symptoms? +

MRI isn't perfect—it misses about 5-10% of ACL tears. If clinical suspicion remains high despite a negative MRI, options include: repeating MRI with a different protocol, diagnostic arthroscopy (camera inside the knee), or clinical observation with physical therapy. Partial tears and chronic tears can sometimes be harder to visualize. Communicate ongoing symptoms to your physician.

Will I need additional tests besides MRI? +

Possibly. Your physician may order: X-rays (to rule out fractures), stress X-rays (to assess ligament laxity), or CT scan (for complex fractures or bone assessment). Blood tests are typically not needed for ACL diagnosis but may be ordered pre-operatively. The specific tests depend on your injury pattern and planned treatment.

How do I prepare for a knee MRI? +

Preparation is minimal: Remove all metal objects (jewelry, watches, piercings). Inform the technician of any metal implants, pacemakers, or claustrophobia. Wear comfortable clothing without metal. You may be given shorts to change into. No fasting required. You can take medications as normal. If claustrophobic, discuss options with your doctor (open MRI, sedation) before the appointment.
