Front Squat

Strength / Squat Intermediate to Advanced Month 4+

Overview

The front squat is a barbell squat variation where the weight is held in front of the body on the shoulders. This position forces a more upright torso, shifts emphasis to the quads, and requires significant core engagement. For ACL rehabilitation, the front squat is excellent for quad development and maintaining good posture during squatting.

Key Benefits

  • Quad dominant: Greater quad emphasis than back squat
  • Upright torso: Forces good posture
  • Self-correcting: Poor form = failed lift (bar falls forward)
  • Core strength: Requires significant core bracing
  • Less spinal compression: Lower loads than back squat
  • Athletic carryover: Similar position to Olympic lifts

Front Rack Position

Clean Grip (Traditional)

  1. Bar rests on front deltoids (shoulders)
  2. Elbows high, pointing forward
  3. Fingertips under bar (not full grip)
  4. Wrists extended back
  5. Requires wrist and lat flexibility

Cross-Arm Grip (Alternative)

  1. Arms crossed in front, hands on opposite shoulders
  2. Bar rests in groove of deltoids
  3. Elbows high to create shelf
  4. Easier for those with limited wrist mobility
  5. Less secure at heavy weights

Strap Grip (Modification)

  1. Loop straps around bar
  2. Hold straps instead of bar
  3. Maintains high elbows
  4. Good for wrist mobility issues

How to Perform

Setup

  1. Set bar in rack at shoulder height
  2. Get under bar, establish front rack position
  3. Unrack by extending legs, step back
  4. Feet shoulder-width, toes slightly out
  5. Big breath, brace core hard

Execution

  1. Initiate descent by breaking at knees and hips simultaneously
  2. Keep elbows high throughout—this is critical
  3. Descend until thighs are below parallel
  4. Maintain upright torso (more vertical than back squat)
  5. Drive up through whole foot
  6. Keep elbows up on the way up
  7. Exhale at top, rebrace for next rep

The "Elbows Up" Cue

If your elbows drop, the bar rolls forward and you lose the lift. The #1 cue for front squats is keeping elbows high. Think "elbows to the ceiling" throughout the movement.

Progressions

Pre-Requisites

  • Solid bodyweight squat with full depth
  • Goblet squat mastered
  • Adequate wrist/lat mobility (or use cross-grip)
  • Usually month 4 at earliest

Level 1: Empty Bar / Light Weight

  • Practice front rack position
  • Learn to keep elbows high
  • Build comfort with the movement
  • 3 sets of 8-10 reps

Level 2: Moderate Loading

  • Progressive weight increases
  • Maintain perfect technique
  • 3 sets of 6-8 reps

Level 3: Strength Focus

  • Heavier weights, lower reps
  • 3-5 sets of 3-5 reps
  • Focus on building max strength

Common Mistakes

Elbows Dropping

Most common error. Bar rolls forward, lift fails. Cue "elbows up" constantly.

Forward Lean

Torso tipping forward, especially out of the hole. Stay more upright than back squat.

Wrist Pain

Gripping bar too tightly or poor rack position. Fingertips only, or use cross grip.

Cutting Depth

Not reaching full depth. Front squats reward deep positions—go below parallel.

Exercise Prescription

Phase Load Sets x Reps Rest
Learning Empty bar 3x8-10 60s
Building Light-Moderate 3x6-8 90s
Strength Moderate-Heavy 3-5x3-5 2-3 min

Frequency: 1-2x per week

Note: Front squat loads are typically 80-85% of back squat loads.

Front Squat vs. Back Squat

Aspect Front Squat Back Squat
Quad emphasis Higher Moderate
Glute/ham emphasis Moderate Higher
Torso angle More upright More forward lean
Core demand Very high High
Max load Lower Higher

Both are valuable. Front squats are particularly useful for quad development and athletes who need upright positions (Olympic lifters, athletes).

Mobility Requirements

Front squats demand:

  • Wrist extension (for clean grip)
  • Lat flexibility
  • Thoracic extension
  • Ankle dorsiflexion

Work on these if front rack position is difficult.

Safety Note

Front squats are safer to bail from than back squats—just dump the bar forward. Use bumper plates and practice bailing with light weight before going heavy.
