Walking & Gait Training

Functional / Mobility Beginner Week 0+

Overview

Restoring a normal walking pattern is one of the first major milestones in ACL recovery. The progression from crutches to independent walking typically takes 2-6 weeks depending on your protocol, graft type, and individual healing. Proper gait mechanics are essential—limping patterns can become habits that cause problems later.

Why Normal Gait Matters

  • Prevents compensation: Limping stresses other joints
  • Quad activation: Walking requires quad function
  • Extension practice: Each step requires full extension
  • Circulation: Movement reduces swelling
  • Mental milestone: Walking freely is empowering
  • Foundation: Running requires good walking first

Weight-Bearing Progression

Follow Your Protocol

Weight-bearing guidelines vary by surgeon and graft type. The progression below is general—always follow your specific post-op instructions.

Phase 1: Toe-Touch Weight Bearing (Days 0-7)

  • Use crutches for all mobility
  • Surgical foot touches ground for balance only
  • No significant weight through surgical leg
  • Brace locked at 0 degrees (per protocol)

Phase 2: Partial Weight Bearing (Week 1-2)

  • Gradually increase weight through surgical leg
  • Use crutches but allow more weight on leg
  • May progress from 25% to 50% to 75% body weight
  • Pain and swelling guide progression

Phase 3: Full Weight Bearing (Week 2-4)

  • Full weight allowed but still use crutches
  • Crutches for safety and confidence
  • Begin focusing on normal gait pattern
  • Usually by week 2-3 for most protocols

Phase 4: Wean Off Crutches (Week 3-6)

  • Transition from 2 crutches to 1
  • Then 1 crutch to no crutches
  • Only when walking without limp
  • Use crutches as long as needed for good gait

Normal Gait Components

What Normal Walking Looks Like

  • Heel strike: Heel contacts ground first
  • Full extension: Knee straight at heel strike
  • Weight shift: Smooth transfer over foot
  • Push-off: Toe pushes off to propel forward
  • Swing through: Leg swings through naturally
  • Equal stride length: Same step size both legs
  • Even timing: Equal time on each leg

Gait Training Exercises

Weight Shifts

  1. Stand with feet shoulder-width, hands on counter for support
  2. Shift weight onto surgical leg
  3. Hold 5-10 seconds
  4. Shift back to non-surgical leg
  5. Repeat 10-15 times

Goal: Full weight on surgical leg with good knee extension

Marching in Place

  1. Stand with support if needed
  2. Lift surgical knee toward chest (as able)
  3. Lower with control
  4. Alternate legs
  5. Focus on equal height and timing
  6. 30 seconds to 1 minute

Tandem Walking (Heel-to-Toe)

  1. Walk in straight line, placing heel directly in front of toes
  2. Arms out for balance if needed
  3. Focus on controlled movement
  4. 10-15 steps forward and back

Backwards Walking

  1. Walk slowly backwards in safe area
  2. Reach back with toe, roll to heel
  3. Requires different muscle activation
  4. Good for quad activation
  5. 10-20 steps, repeat

Lateral Walking (Sidestepping)

  1. Step sideways with surgical leg leading
  2. Bring feet together
  3. Continue in one direction
  4. Repeat leading with non-surgical leg
  5. 10-15 steps each direction

Common Gait Problems

Quad Avoidance Gait

Not fully extending knee, walking with bent knee. Focus on full extension at heel strike.

Shortened Stride

Taking smaller steps on surgical side. Consciously match stride length.

Trunk Lean

Leaning away from surgical leg. Stand tall, equal weight both sides.

Hip Hiking

Lifting hip to swing leg through. Work on quad strength and hip flexor flexibility.

Rushing Off Crutches

Ditching crutches while still limping. Use them until gait is normal.

Walking Progression

Week 3-4 1 crutch → none Independent walking Regular walking
Week 4+ None Outdoor terrain, stairs Progressive distance

Stair Walking

Stairs are introduced once level walking is comfortable:

  • Up: Good leg leads ("up with the good")
  • Down: Surgical leg leads ("down with the bad")
  • Use railing: For safety and confidence
  • Step-to pattern: Both feet on each step initially
  • Reciprocal: Alternate feet as strength improves (usually month 2+)

The Limp Rule

If you're limping, use support. A limp means you're not ready to walk unassisted. Using crutches longer to achieve normal gait is better than ditching them early and developing bad habits.

Crutch Tip

When using 1 crutch, hold it on the opposite side from your surgical leg. This mimics natural arm swing and provides better support.
