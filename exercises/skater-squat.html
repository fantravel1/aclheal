Skater Squat

Strength / Single-Leg Advanced Month 5+

Overview

The skater squat (or airborne lunge) is an advanced single-leg exercise where you lower onto one leg while the other leg floats behind you. Unlike Bulgarian split squats, there's no bench support—your back leg provides no assistance. This makes it extremely challenging for balance, strength, and coordination.

Why Skater Squats for ACL?

  • True single-leg strength test
  • No equipment needed
  • Excellent limb symmetry assessment
  • Builds sport-relevant single-leg power
  • Challenges proprioception significantly

Key Benefits

  • Pure single-leg: No back leg assistance
  • No equipment: Can do anywhere
  • Balance intensive: Major proprioceptive challenge
  • Quad dominant: Similar demand to pistol squat
  • Symmetry marker: Shows differences between legs

How to Perform

Setup

  1. Stand on one leg, other leg bent behind you
  2. Arms extended forward for counterbalance
  3. Working leg slightly bent at start
  4. Core engaged, chest up

Execution

  1. Slowly lower body by bending working leg
  2. Back knee travels down and slightly back
  3. Lower until back knee gently touches ground (or pad)
  4. Keep torso as upright as possible
  5. Drive through working leg to stand back up
  6. Maintain balance throughout

Key Form Points

  • Arms forward: Counterbalance is essential
  • Slow descent: Control is more important than speed
  • Knee pad: Use pad for back knee to touch
  • Upright torso: Minimize forward lean
  • Full range: Touch knee down for complete rep

Progressions

Prerequisites

  • Solid Bulgarian split squat with weight
  • Good single-leg balance (30+ seconds)
  • Step-downs with control
  • Usually Month 5+ at earliest

Level 1: Assisted Skater Squat

  • Hold TRX, band, or pole for support
  • Use minimal assistance—just for balance
  • Learn the movement pattern
  • 3 sets of 5-6 each leg

Level 2: Elevated Target

  • Stack pads or mats as target for back knee
  • Reduces range of motion
  • Progress by lowering target height
  • 3 sets of 6-8 each leg

Level 3: Full Bodyweight

  • No assistance, knee to ground
  • Use thin pad for knee comfort
  • 3 sets of 6-8 each leg

Level 4: Weighted Skater Squat

  • Hold weight at chest (goblet style)
  • Or hold dumbbells at sides
  • Progress weight gradually
  • 3 sets of 6-8 each leg

Common Mistakes

Falling Forward

Losing balance forward. Extend arms more, slow down descent.

Back Leg Pushing

Using back leg to help stand up. Keep back leg completely passive.

Knee Valgus

Working knee caving in. Push knee out over toes.

Rushing

Moving too fast. Slow and controlled is the goal.

Skater Squat vs. Pistol Squat

Aspect Skater Squat Pistol Squat
Non-working leg Behind, knee bent In front, extended
Mobility demand Moderate Very high (ankle/hip)
Balance Very challenging Extremely challenging
ACL-friendly Yes Caution needed

For ACL rehab, the skater squat is generally preferred as it requires less extreme ankle mobility and keeps the knee in a more controlled position.

Symmetry Test

Can you do equal reps with equal control on both legs? Differences between sides indicate remaining strength or neuromuscular deficits to address before return to sport.

When Ready?

Prerequisites before attempting:

  • 10+ reps Bulgarian split squat
  • Good step-down control
  • 30+ sec single-leg balance
  • No pain or apprehension
