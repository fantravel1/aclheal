Squats

Strength / Fundamental Beginner to Advanced Month 2+

Overview

The squat is the foundational lower body movement pattern. After ACL surgery, rebuilding the ability to squat safely and deeply is a major milestone. Squats develop quad, glute, and core strength while training the entire lower body to work as a unit. Proper squat mechanics are essential for athletic movements and daily activities.

Key Benefits

  • Functional movement: Sitting, standing, jumping all require this pattern
  • Quad and glute strength: Primary targets for ACL rehab
  • Closed-chain: Foot fixed, protective for the knee
  • Highly scalable: Bodyweight to heavy barbell
  • Full body engagement: Core, back, and legs work together
  • Mobility requirement: Develops and maintains hip/ankle flexibility

Bodyweight Squat

Setup

  1. Feet shoulder-width or slightly wider
  2. Toes pointed slightly outward (15-30 degrees)
  3. Arms extended forward or crossed at chest
  4. Weight balanced across entire foot

Execution

  1. Initiate by pushing hips back slightly
  2. Bend knees and lower body down
  3. Keep chest up and back neutral
  4. Descend until thighs are parallel or below
  5. Knees track over toes throughout
  6. Drive through whole foot to stand
  7. Squeeze glutes at the top

Key Form Points

  • Knees out: Don't let knees collapse inward
  • Heels down: If heels lift, work on ankle mobility
  • Neutral spine: No excessive rounding or arching
  • Depth: As deep as form allows (parallel is good baseline)
  • Even pressure: Equal weight on both legs

Progression Roadmap

Level 1: Mini Squats (Month 1-2)

  • Partial range (45-60 degree knee bend)
  • Focus on alignment and control
  • Wall or counter support if needed
  • 3 sets of 10-15 reps

Level 2: Box Squat (Month 2-3)

  • Squat to bench or box
  • Sit briefly, then stand
  • Lower box height as able
  • 3 sets of 10-12 reps

Level 3: Bodyweight Squat (Month 2-3)

  • Full depth (thighs parallel or below)
  • No support, controlled throughout
  • Focus on symmetry between legs
  • 3 sets of 12-15 reps

Level 4: Goblet Squat (Month 3-4)

  • Hold dumbbell or kettlebell at chest
  • Weight promotes upright posture
  • Progress load gradually
  • 3 sets of 10-12 reps

Level 5: Front Squat (Month 4+)

  • Barbell in front rack position
  • Requires mobility and technique
  • Very quad-dominant
  • 3-4 sets of 6-10 reps

Level 6: Back Squat (Month 4+)

  • Barbell on upper back (high or low bar)
  • Allows heaviest loading
  • Standard strength exercise
  • 3-5 sets of 5-10 reps

Squat Variations

Pause Squat

2-3 second hold at bottom. Builds strength out of the hole, eliminates momentum.

Tempo Squat

Slow descent (3-4 seconds). Increases time under tension and control.

Heels Elevated

Heels on small plates or wedge. Helps those with limited ankle mobility.

Sumo Squat

Wide stance, toes out. More adductor and glute emphasis.

1.5 Rep Squat

Full squat, halfway up, back down, then stand. Doubles bottom-range work.

Common Mistakes

Knee Valgus

Knees caving inward. Cue "knees out" and strengthen hip abductors.

Forward Lean

Torso falling forward excessively. Keep chest up, may need ankle/hip mobility work.

Butt Wink

Lower back rounding at bottom. Don't go deeper than form allows.

Heels Rising

Coming onto toes. Work on ankle mobility or elevate heels temporarily.

Asymmetric Shift

Favoring non-surgical leg. Watch in mirror, cue equal weight distribution.

ACL-Specific Considerations

  • Knee position: Research shows knees traveling past toes is not inherently harmful—focus on control and alignment
  • Depth: Full depth is fine when ready; don't force it early in rehab
  • Symmetry: Work toward equal contribution from both legs
  • Pain-free: Some discomfort is normal; sharp pain is not—adjust accordingly
  • Progress gradually: Master bodyweight before adding load

Squat Depth Myth

"Don't squat below parallel" is outdated advice. Research shows full-depth squats are safe when performed correctly, and may even be protective for the knee by building strength through full ROM.

Mobility Requirements

  • Ankle dorsiflexion (knee over toes)
  • Hip flexion (thighs to torso)
  • Thoracic extension (upright torso)

Address limitations with mobility work before forcing depth.
