Hamstring Curl Machine

Strength Beginner to Advanced Week 4+

Overview

The hamstring curl machine is a foundational exercise for rebuilding hamstring strength after ACL surgery. Hamstrings are dynamic stabilizers of the knee—they resist anterior tibial translation, working synergistically with the ACL. Strong hamstrings reduce stress on your graft.

Hamstring Graft Patients

If you had a hamstring graft, your hamstrings were weakened by the harvest. This exercise is especially important for you, but progress very gradually. Your PT may recommend delaying isolated hamstring curls until week 6-8 to protect the harvest site.

Key Benefits

  • Isolated hamstring work: Targets hamstrings without quad involvement
  • Controlled resistance: Machine provides consistent load
  • Easy to progress: Small weight increments available
  • Safe positioning: Stable setup, no balance demands
  • ACL protection: Strong hamstrings reduce graft strain

Machine Types

Prone (Lying) Curl

Lie face down, curl heels toward glutes. Most common type. Good for full ROM.

Seated Curl

Sit upright, curl heels under seat. May be more comfortable for some. Slightly different muscle emphasis.

Standing Curl

One leg at a time, standing. Adds balance component. Less common in gyms.

Prone Hamstring Curl

Setup

  1. Lie face down on machine pad
  2. Position ankle pad just above heels (on Achilles area)
  3. Align knee joint with machine pivot point
  4. Grip handles for stability
  5. Keep hips pressed into pad throughout

Execution

  1. Start with legs extended (slight bend is fine)
  2. Curl heels toward glutes by bending knees
  3. Squeeze hamstrings at top of movement
  4. Lower with control (2-3 seconds)
  5. Don't let weight stack touch between reps

Form Checkpoints

  • ✓ Hips stay pressed down (no lifting)
  • ✓ Ankles stay neutral (don't point toes)
  • ✓ Full ROM: from near extension to full flexion
  • ✓ Control the eccentric (lowering) phase

Progression Protocol

Phase 1: Light Weight, High Rep (Week 4-6)

  • Very light weight (may feel "too easy")
  • 3 sets of 15-20 reps
  • Focus on form and muscle connection
  • Bilateral (both legs together)

Phase 2: Building Load (Week 6-10)

  • Gradually increase weight
  • 3 sets of 12-15 reps
  • Should feel challenging on last 2-3 reps
  • Still bilateral

Phase 3: Single Leg Introduction (Week 10+)

  • Reduce weight by 40-50%
  • Perform with surgical leg only
  • 3 sets of 10-12 reps
  • Compare to non-surgical leg

Phase 4: Strength Building (Month 3+)

  • Progressive overload: increase weight when reps become easy
  • 3-4 sets of 8-12 reps
  • Goal: surgical leg matches non-surgical

Common Mistakes

Hips Lifting

Hips rising off pad during curl. Use lighter weight and focus on keeping hips pressed down.

Using Momentum

Swinging weight up instead of controlled curl. Slow down the movement.

Partial ROM

Not going through full range. Aim for near-full extension to full flexion.

Dropping Weight

Letting weight fall on eccentric. Control the lowering for 2-3 seconds.

Hamstring:Quad Ratio

Research suggests hamstring strength should be at least 60-70% of quad strength for knee health. Many ACL patients have ratios below this—hamstring work is essential.

Hamstring Graft Note

Your surgeon may restrict isolated hamstring exercises for the first 6-8 weeks to protect the harvest site. Follow your specific protocol. When cleared, start very light.
