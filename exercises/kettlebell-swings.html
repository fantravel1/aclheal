Kettlebell Swings

Power / Hip Hinge Intermediate Month 4+

Overview

The kettlebell swing is a ballistic hip hinge movement that develops explosive hip extension power. It bridges the gap between strength training and plyometrics, making it valuable for ACL rehabilitation when preparing for return to sport. The movement trains the posterior chain explosively while being relatively low-impact on the knee.

Key Benefits

  • Hip power: Explosive hip extension for running/jumping
  • Posterior chain: Glutes, hamstrings, back all engaged
  • Cardiovascular: High heart rate with minimal knee stress
  • Low impact: Feet stay planted (vs. jumping)
  • Core strength: Anti-extension challenge
  • Efficient: Full-body exercise in minimal time

Russian Swing (Standard)

Setup

  1. Stand with feet slightly wider than shoulder-width
  2. Kettlebell on floor about 12 inches in front
  3. Hinge at hips, slight knee bend
  4. Grip kettlebell handle with both hands
  5. Shoulders pulled back, lats engaged
  6. Neutral spine, eyes forward

The Hike

  1. Tilt kettlebell toward you
  2. Hike it back between legs like a football snap
  3. Keep arms connected to inner thighs
  4. Load the hips and hamstrings

The Swing

  1. Explosively extend hips forward
  2. Squeeze glutes hard at the top
  3. Arms are just along for the ride—don't lift with shoulders
  4. Kettlebell floats to chest/shoulder height
  5. Let gravity bring it back down
  6. Hinge at hips to absorb, immediately reverse
  7. Maintain continuous rhythm

Key Concept: It's a Hinge, Not a Squat

The swing is powered by the hips, not the knees. Think "hip snap" not "squat and lift." The knee bend is minimal—this is a hip-dominant movement. If you're squatting, you're doing it wrong.

Variations

Deadlift to Swing (Learning)

  • Practice kettlebell deadlift first
  • Add small swing at top
  • Gradually increase swing height
  • Progress to continuous swings

Two-Hand Swing (Standard)

  • Both hands on handle
  • Most stable version
  • Best for learning and heavy weight

Single-Arm Swing (Advanced)

  • One hand on handle
  • Adds rotational challenge
  • Greater core demand
  • Addresses asymmetries

American Swing (High Swing)

  • Kettlebell goes overhead
  • Common in CrossFit
  • More shoulder involvement
  • Not recommended for ACL rehab—stick with Russian

Common Mistakes

Squatting the Swing

Too much knee bend, not enough hip hinge. Hips go back, knees stay relatively straight.

Lifting with Arms

Using shoulders to raise kettlebell. Arms are ropes—power comes from hips.

Rounded Back

Lower back rounding during hinge. Maintain neutral spine throughout.

Not Finishing Tall

Leaning back at top. Stand tall with glutes squeezed, not hyperextended.

Early Arm Bend

Bending arms on the way up. Keep arms straight until lockout.

Exercise Prescription

Phase Weight Volume Rest
Learning Light (8-12 kg) 3x10-15 60s
Building Moderate (12-20 kg) 4x15-20 45-60s
Power Moderate-Heavy 5x10-15 60-90s
Conditioning Moderate Timed sets (30s on/30s off) As prescribed

Frequency: 2-3x per week

Weight Selection

Starting recommendations (adjust based on strength):

  • Women, new to KB: 8-12 kg (18-26 lbs)
  • Men, new to KB: 12-16 kg (26-35 lbs)
  • Experienced: 16-24+ kg (35-53+ lbs)

When in doubt, start lighter. Technique matters more than weight.

Pre-Plyometric Power

Kettlebell swings are excellent preparation for jumping and plyometrics. They train explosive hip extension without the landing impact, making them a great bridge exercise in ACL rehab.

The "Hip Snap"

The power comes from explosively snapping the hips forward. Imagine you're trying to jump forward but keeping your feet planted. That hip drive is what moves the kettlebell.
