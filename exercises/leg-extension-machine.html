Leg Extension Machine

Quad Strength Beginner to Advanced Week 6+ (with ROM limits)

Overview

The leg extension machine isolates the quadriceps through knee extension. While effective for building quad strength, it requires careful ROM (range of motion) management during ACL recovery because open-chain knee extension places stress on the ACL graft in certain ranges.

ROM Considerations

The key concern: Open-chain knee extension from 45° to 0° (full extension) creates anterior tibial translation that stresses the ACL. Most protocols restrict this range early in recovery.

  • Weeks 0-6: Many protocols avoid leg extensions entirely
  • Weeks 6-12: Limited ROM (90°-45° is generally safe)
  • Week 12+: Full ROM may be permitted

Always follow your surgeon/PT protocol.

Key Benefits

  • Quad isolation: Pure quad work without other muscle involvement
  • Easy to load: Simple weight progression
  • Measurable progress: Clear strength tracking
  • Safe setup: No balance demands
  • Addresses quad atrophy: Directly targets the muscle most affected by surgery

Setup

Machine Positioning

  1. Adjust back pad so knees align with machine pivot point
  2. Position ankle pad just above ankles (on lower shin)
  3. Sit fully back with back against pad
  4. Grip handles at sides
  5. Set ROM limiters if available

ROM Limiter Setup

Many machines have adjustable stops. Set these to limit extension to your allowed range:

  • Conservative (weeks 6-12): Stop at 45° from full extension
  • Moderate (weeks 12-16): Stop at 30° from full extension
  • Full ROM (week 16+): Allow near-complete extension

Execution

Limited ROM Leg Extension (Weeks 6-12)

  1. Start with knees bent 90°
  2. Extend knees to 45° (halfway to straight)
  3. Squeeze quads at top of allowed range
  4. Lower slowly back to 90°
  5. Control the movement in both directions

Mental cue: You're working the middle range only—don't chase full extension yet.

Progressive ROM (Week 12+)

  1. Gradually increase extension range
  2. Week 12-14: 90° to 30°
  3. Week 14-16: 90° to 15°
  4. Week 16+: Near full ROM (per protocol)

Full ROM (When Cleared)

  1. Full range from 90° flexion to near-complete extension
  2. Squeeze hard at top for 1-2 seconds
  3. Lower with control
  4. Progress weight as strength improves

Single Leg Progression

Bilateral vs. Unilateral

  • Start bilateral: Both legs together reduces load per leg
  • Progress to single leg: Once comfortable, train surgical leg alone
  • Compare sides: Track strength difference between legs
  • Goal: Less than 10% difference between legs

Common Mistakes

Ignoring ROM Limits

Going to full extension before cleared. Respect your protocol's ROM guidelines.

Too Much Weight Too Soon

Loading heavily before adequate healing. Start light, progress gradually.

Lifting Butt Off Seat

Coming off the seat to complete reps. Reduce weight and stay seated.

Fast, Jerky Movements

Using momentum instead of muscle. Slow, controlled reps are safer and more effective.

Exercise Prescription

Phase ROM Sets x Reps Intensity
Week 6-10 90°-45° 3x15-20 Light
Week 10-12 90°-30° 3x12-15 Moderate
Week 12-16 90°-15° 3x10-12 Moderate
Week 16+ Full 3-4x8-12 Progressive

Frequency: 2-3x per week

Safer Alternatives

If you're not yet cleared for leg extensions, these closed-chain exercises are generally safer:

Leg Press

Quad strengthening without open-chain stress

Mini Squats

Functional quad work

TKE

Terminal extension with band

Protocol Variance

Leg extension guidelines vary significantly between surgeons and protocols. Some allow earlier with ROM limits; others avoid entirely for 3-4 months. Follow your specific instructions.

Open vs. Closed Chain

  • Open chain: Foot moves freely (leg extension)
  • Closed chain: Foot fixed (squat, leg press)

Closed chain is generally safer for ACL rehab because co-contraction of hamstrings protects the joint.
